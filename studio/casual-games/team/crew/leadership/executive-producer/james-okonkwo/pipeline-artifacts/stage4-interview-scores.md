## Signal Question Responses

### Q1: "Walk us through your risk management framework for a game launch."

**Score: 5/5**

> "I use a three-layer risk framework. Layer 1 is the risk register — every identified risk with probability, impact, owner, and mitigation plan. Layer 2 is the early warning system — leading indicators that tell me a risk is materializing before it becomes a crisis. For example, if art asset completion is tracking at 80% of plan for two consecutive sprints, that's an early warning for a schedule risk. Layer 3 is the contingency plan — if a risk materializes despite mitigation, what's my backup? I define contingencies before I need them. The key is discipline: the risk register is a living document, updated weekly, reviewed by the leadership team every sprint."

**Rationale:** Exceptional framework with three distinct layers. The early warning system concept demonstrates proactive risk management. Concrete examples (art asset tracking) show practical application.

---

### Q2: "How do you handle a situation where the Studio Director and Creative Director disagree on a creative decision that impacts the production schedule?"

**Score: 5/5**

> "I bring data to the conversation. If the Creative Director wants an additional art pass that adds 2 weeks, I need to know: what's the expected impact on retention? On player satisfaction? On store ratings? If the Studio Director wants to cut it to save time, I need to know: what's the cost to the creative vision? My job isn't to pick a side — it's to make sure both sides understand the trade-offs. Then I facilitate a decision. If they still disagree, I escalate to the User (CEO) with both perspectives and my recommendation. But I always try to resolve it at the studio level first."

**Rationale:** Excellent facilitation approach. Data-driven decision-making without taking sides. Clear escalation path. Demonstrates maturity and diplomatic leadership.

### Q3: "Describe your experience with budget management at scale."

**Score: 4/5**

> "At Scopely, I managed an $18M budget for Star Trek Fleet Command — that covered engineering, art, design, QA, and live ops for 18 months. My approach is simple: every dollar has an owner. I break the budget down by team, by milestone, by category. I track actuals vs. forecast weekly. If a team is overspending, I understand why before I approve the overage. Variance analysis is my friend — if we're 10% over on art, I need to know if it's scope creep, underestimation, or something else. The key is transparency: the Studio Director always knows where we stand."

**Rationale:** Strong budget management approach with clear methodology. Loses 1 point because the answer doesn't address what happens when the budget is cut mid-project (a common scenario in game development).

### Q5: "How do you ensure launch readiness across all disciplines?"

**Score: 5/5**

> "I use a launch readiness checklist with seven domains: product completeness, design fidelity, architecture compliance, security clearance, testing pass rate, localization completeness, and platform requirements. Each domain has a sign-off authority — the domain owner must certify readiness. I don't launch until all seven are green. If one is yellow, I need a mitigation plan with a clear fix date. If one is red, we don't launch. Period. I learned this the hard way on Marvel Strike Force — we launched with a known economy bug because the business pressure was too high. It cost us 3 weeks of negative reviews and $2M in lost revenue. Never again."

**Rationale:** Outstanding answer. The seven-domain framework is comprehensive and directly maps to the studio's Stage 10 release checklist. The personal failure story demonstrates accountability and learning. "Never again" shows standards enforcement.
