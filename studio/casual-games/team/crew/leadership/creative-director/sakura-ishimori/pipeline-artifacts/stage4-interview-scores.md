## Signal Question Responses

### Q1: "Walk us through how you designed the 'master scene' art direction for Royal Match."

**Score: 5/5**

> "The 'master scene' concept came from a simple observation: players spend 80% of their time on the level map. So the map isn't just navigation — it's the game's living room. I treated it like a film set. Every element tells a story. The mansion renovation progresses as you progress. The garden changes with seasons. The lighting shifts from morning to evening based on real time. It's not decoration — it's emotional investment. Players don't just complete levels; they build a world. That's why retention is so high. People come back to see their world grow."

**Rationale:** Exceptional insight into player psychology and environmental storytelling. The "living room" metaphor demonstrates deep understanding of emotional engagement. Direct connection between creative decisions and retention metrics.

---

### Q2: "How do you handle the tension between creative ambition and technical constraints?"

**Score: 4/5**

> "I work with engineering from Day 1, not after the art is done. We agree on performance budgets together — polycount, texture size, animation complexity. Then I design within those constraints. The best creative solutions come from constraints, not from ignoring them. That said, if a creative element is truly a 'non-negotiable' for the player experience, I'll push engineering to find a way. But I always come with data — not opinions. If I can show that a specific visual element increases D1 retention by 5%, engineering will find a way to make it work."

**Rationale:** Strong collaborative approach with engineering. Data-driven persuasion is excellent. Loses 1 point because the answer doesn't address what happens when engineering genuinely cannot deliver (e.g., hardware limitations on low-end devices).

---

### Q3: "Describe your approach to IAP monetization design."

**Score: 5/5**

> "I believe in IAP-only monetization — no ads, ever. Ads break the immersion and tell players their time isn't valued. My approach is simple: every IAP should feel like a natural extension of the fun. If a player wants to buy a boost, it's because they're invested in the level, not because they're frustrated. I design the economy so that skilled players can progress without paying, but impatient players can pay to go faster. The key is balance — if paying feels like cheating, free players leave. If paying feels pointless, paying players leave. The sweet spot is in the middle."

**Rationale:** Clear, principled monetization philosophy. Player-first approach aligns perfectly with studio values. The "skilled vs. impatient" framework demonstrates deep economic design understanding.

---

### Q4: "How would you scale your creative process for a 50+ person creative organization?"

**Score: 3/5**

> "Honestly, I haven't led a team that large. At Dream Games, my creative team was about 15 people. For 50+, I'd need to shift from hands-on creative direction to creative governance. I'd establish creative leads for each discipline — art, design, narrative — and define clear quality gates. My role becomes ensuring all disciplines share the same visual pillars and player experience goals. I'd also invest heavily in documentation and tooling so that quality standards are baked into the pipeline, not dependent on my personal review."

**Rationale:** Honest and self-aware. Acknowledges the gap directly. The governance framework is reasonable but untested at that scale. This is a genuine development area.

---

### Q5: "What's your creative process for defining visual pillars?"

**Score: 5/5**

> "I start with emotion, not aesthetics. What should the player feel? For Royal Match, it was 'warmth' — like visiting your grandmother's house. Everything flowed from that. The color palette was warm tones. The animations were gentle, not snappy. The UI had rounded corners, not sharp edges. Even the sound design was soft and comforting. I write three words — only three — and every creative decision must pass the test: 'Does this feel warm?' If it doesn't, it doesn't ship. Three words is enough. More than that and you're writing a dictionary, not a vision."

**Rationale:** Outstanding creative process description. The emotion-first approach, three-word framework, and concrete Royal Match examples demonstrate world-class creative direction methodology.
